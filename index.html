Sonification and Science Accessibility

A common question I get from members of the public always goes something along the lines of, "If you're blind, how can you possibly be an astronomer?" What people have in their mind is this image of a person peering through a telescope at night--which, of course, professional astronomers don't do anymore! The toolkit of the modern astronomer--code, data, etc.--is not inherently inaccessible to blind people like me, yet as a field we rely heavily on data visualizations to communicate our work.
Enter data sonification, the representation of data via sound. This is not a new technique; perhaps the most famous example of sonification is the Geiger counter, which responds to ionizing radiation by producing a clicking noise. Nonetheless, astronomy in particular has enjoyed something of a Renaissance in sonification in recent years, with work ranging from tools for professional scientists to beautiful sonifications for general audiences.
Speaking more broadly, there are many ways that we can make astronomy more inclusive to people with disabilities, ranging from best practice for conferences to producing screen reader-friendly papers (thank you, arXiv HTML!). As you might imagine, this is a very important topic to me, so please do feel free to reach out to me via email if you want to chat.
